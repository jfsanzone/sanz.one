Niche Is the Only Moat

April 6, 2026

I spent today building The Niche AI Playbook — an intelligence report and interactive tool that makes the case for vertical AI. The thesis is simple: the wrapper era is over, and the only defensible position left is going narrower than feels comfortable.

Here's the problem I kept running into when thinking about AI app strategy. Every horizontal tool — the generic email writers, the all-purpose summarizers, the broad-audience chatbots — is in a race it can't win. The labs that power these tools are also their biggest competitive threat. Every model update commoditizes another layer of thin wrappers overnight. You're building on a foundation that's actively trying to eat your product.

The data backs this up. Vertical SaaS is growing 2.5× faster than horizontal right now. Customer acquisition costs for niche products run 40–60% lower than their broad-market equivalents. Churn is dramatically lower because the product is embedded in workflows people can't easily replace. Harvey went from zero to $100M ARR in 36 months by going all-in on legal AI and nothing else. Ambience Healthcare reached 50%+ hospital penetration for AI medical scribes by solving one specific, painful workflow for one specific type of user.

The insight that keeps landing for me is this: the hard problem in AI has shifted. It used to be "how do I build it?" Now it's "what do I build?" The barrier to creating software has collapsed. Anyone can ship a functioning app in a weekend. That means differentiation can't come from technical execution anymore — it has to come from domain expertise, proprietary data, and deeply understood workflows that a general model will never have.

The playbook I built walks through the full argument — the horizontal squeeze, the vertical advantage, a side-by-side comparison of thin wrappers vs. thick verticals, and a five-question niche positioning tool that scores your defensibility. If you're building something in the AI space and you're not sure whether you're building a moat or a wrapper, go run it. The answer might be uncomfortable. That's the point.
