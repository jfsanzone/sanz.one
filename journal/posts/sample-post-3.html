What Product Leaders Get Wrong About Speed

January 12, 2026

In almost every product org I've worked with, "velocity" has become a synonym for value. Ship faster, iterate more, compress the cycle. The logic feels airtight: the faster you ship, the faster you learn, the faster you win. But there's a version of this that goes wrong, quietly and expensively, and most leaders don't notice until the damage is already structural.

The failure mode isn't slowness. It's incoherence. Teams optimized purely for speed produce a lot of output that doesn't compound. Features launch, metrics briefly move, and then the product accretes another layer of complexity that makes the next feature harder to build, harder to test, and harder to explain to a new user. Velocity without direction is just expensive thrashing. The fastest teams I've seen weren't fast because they cut corners. They were fast because they'd already agreed on where they were going.

What the best product leaders understand is that speed is an output, not an input. You don't get faster by demanding speed. You get faster by removing ambiguity: by writing a strategy clear enough that individual teams can make local decisions without escalation, by investing in design systems and platform work that eliminate redundant effort, by saying no to the seven mediocre ideas so you can say yes to the one that matters. The real leverage isn't doing more things faster. It's doing fewer things with more conviction.
